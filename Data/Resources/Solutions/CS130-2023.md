1) For each part of the question, find an undirected simple graph $G = (V, E)$ with the specified properties, or prove that none exists.


**(a)** $G$ is a tree in which no vertex has degree $1$.

>[!hint]- Hint 1
> Recall the definition: a tree is a **connected, acyclic** graph. The definition says nothing about minimum degree.

>[!hint]- Hint 2
> Does a tree have to have every vertex of degree $\geq 1$? Think about the *smallest* tree you can possibly write down.

>[!check]- Solution
> Yes, take the trivial graph $G = (\{v\}, \emptyset)$, a single vertex with no edges.
>
> $G$ is connected (trivially), acyclic (no edges, hence no cycles), so it is a tree. Its only vertex has degree $0$, which is not $1$, so no vertex of $G$ has degree $1$.
>
> *Note: for any tree with $\geq 2$ vertices, a standard counting argument shows the tree has at least $2$ leaves (vertices of degree $1$). So this single-vertex tree is essentially the only example.*


**(b)** $G$ is a tree which has $5$ connected components and $|V| = 10$.

>[!hint]- Hint 1
> Recall the definition: a tree is a **connected, acyclic** graph. How many connected components does a connected graph have?

>[!check]- Solution
> No such graph exists.
>
> A tree is connected by definition, so it has exactly **one** connected component. A graph with $5$ connected components cannot be a tree.


**(c)** $G$ is non-planar, $|V| = 10$, and $|E| = 8$.

>[!hint]- Hint 1
> Recall **Euler's formula** for connected planar graphs: $|V| - |E| + |F| = 2$, where $|F|$ is the number of faces.

>[!hint]- Hint 2
> $|F| \geq 1$ for any planar embedding: every embedding has at least one face, the unbounded outer face.

>[!check]- Solution
> No such graph exists.
>
> By Euler's formula for connected planar graphs, $|V| - |E| + |F| = 2$. Substituting $|V| = 10$ and $|E| = 8$:
> $|V| - |E| + |F| = 10 - 8 + |F| = 2 + |F|$.
>
> Since $|F| \geq 1$ (any planar embedding has at least one face: the unbounded outer face), $2 + |F| \geq 3 > 2$, contradicting Euler's $|V| - |E| + |F| = 2$. Hence no such graph exists.


**(d)** $G$ has $5$ connected components, $|V| = 10$, and $|E| = 9$.

>[!hint]- Hint 1
> The minimum number of edges needed to have $C$ connected components on $V$ vertices is $V - C$ (a spanning forest of $C$ trees). Anything beyond that goes into making one or more components "thicker" than a tree.

>[!hint]- Hint 2
> With $V = 10$ and $C = 5$, you need at least $V - C = 5$ edges. You have $9$ to spend, so $4$ edges go on top of a spanning forest. Try keeping most components as isolated vertices and putting all the edges into one component.

>[!check]- Solution
> Yes, take $4$ isolated vertices together with one copy of $K_{3,3}$ (the complete bipartite graph on $3 + 3 = 6$ vertices, which has $9$ edges).
>
> This gives $5$ connected components ($4$ singletons plus one $K_{3,3}$), $|V| = 4 + 6 = 10$, and $|E| = 0 + 9 = 9$.


**(e)** $G$ contains an Eulerian cycle, $|V| = 10$, and $|E| > 40$.

>[!hint]- Hint 1
> Recall: a connected graph has an Eulerian cycle iff every vertex has **even** degree.

>[!hint]- Hint 2
> The maximum number of edges in a simple graph on $10$ vertices is $\binom{10}{2} = 45$, achieved by $K_{10}$. To get $|E| > 40$, we need to remove fewer than $5$ edges from $K_{10}$. What does removing an edge do to the parity of each vertex's degree?

>[!check]- Solution
> No such graph exists.
>
> Start from $K_{10}$ (every node connected to every other node), which has $\binom{10}{2} = 45$ edges and every vertex has degree $9$ (odd). This has the maximum number of edges possible with $10$ vertices: any simple graph on $10$ vertices with $|E| > 40$ is obtained from $K_{10}$ by removing at most $4$ edges. So the goal will be to remove edges, ensuring $|E| > 40$, and achieving every vertex having even degree. 
>
> Removing an edge $\{u, v\}$ decreases the degrees of both $u$ and $v$ by $1$, flipping their parities (from odd to even). To make every vertex of $G$ have even degree, we'd need to flip the parity of all $10$ vertices, so each vertex must be incident to an *odd* number (in particular, at least one) of the removed edges.
>
> We need to flip all $10$ vertices, which means we need to eliminate at least $5$ edges. If we eliminate $5$ edges, then we achieve $|E| \leq 45 - 5 = 40$ edges. 
>
> Hence $|E| \leq 45 - 5 = 40$, contradicting $|E| > 40$. So no such graph exists.

2) **(a)** Write a compound proposition that is true if and only if at most one of the propositions $p, q, r$ is true.

>[!hint]- Hint 1
> "At most one true" means $F$ or $T$ of $p, q, r$ are true. Split your formula into those two cases and OR them.

>[!hint]- Hint 2
> "Zero true" is easy: $\neg p \land \neg q \land \neg r$. For "exactly one true" you need three sub-cases (which of $p, q, r$ is the true one), each with the other two negated.

>[!check]- Solution
> Split into "all false" OR "exactly one true":
> $(\neg p \land \neg q \land \neg r) \lor (p \land \neg q \land \neg r) \lor (\neg p \land q \land \neg r) \lor (\neg p \land \neg q \land r)$.
>
> A cleaner equivalent (but harder to think up) is "no two of the three are simultaneously true":
> $\neg((p \land q) \lor (p \land r) \lor (q \land r))$.


**(b)** Let $C$ denote the compound proposition $(p \lor \neg q) \land (q \lor \neg p)$. Is the proposition $C \to (p \leftrightarrow q)$ a tautology?

>[!hint]- Hint 1
> Recall the implication identity $a \to b \;\equiv\; \neg a \lor b$. Equivalently, $b \to a \;\equiv\; a \lor \neg b$. Can you rewrite each of $(p \lor \neg q)$ and $(q \lor \neg p)$ as an implication?

>[!hint]- Hint 2
> $(p \lor \neg q)$ is $q \to p$, and $(q \lor \neg p)$ is $p \to q$. So $C$ is the conjunction of those two, what is $(q \to p) \land (p \to q)$?

>[!check]- Solution
> Yes, $C \to (p \leftrightarrow q)$ is a tautology.
>
> Using $a \lor \neg b \equiv b \to a$:
> - $(p \lor \neg q) \equiv (q \to p)$
> - $(q \lor \neg p) \equiv (p \to q)$
>
> So $C \equiv (q \to p) \land (p \to q) \equiv (p \leftrightarrow q)$.
>
> Hence $C \to (p \leftrightarrow q)$ is just $(p \leftrightarrow q) \to (p \leftrightarrow q)$, which is true under every assignment (any formula $\phi \to \phi$ is a tautology).


**(c)** Find an infinite subset $K$ of $\mathbb{Z}$, different from $\mathbb{Z}$, such that the logical formula $\forall a \forall b \exists c.\, a + c = b$ is true when all three variables range over $K$.

>[!hint]- Hint 1
> Solving for $c$: the formula says $c = b - a$. So $K$ must be closed under taking differences, for any $a, b \in K$, $b - a$ must also lie in $K$. What familiar infinite proper subset of $\mathbb{Z}$ is closed under subtraction?

>[!check]- Solution
> Take $K = 2\mathbb{Z} = \{\ldots, -4, -2, 0, 2, 4, \ldots\}$, the even integers.
>
> For any $a, b \in K$, set $c = b - a$. The difference of two even numbers is even, so $c \in K$, and $a + c = a + (b - a) = b$. So $\forall a \forall b \exists c.\, a + c = b$ holds in $K$.
>
> $K$ is infinite, and $K \neq \mathbb{Z}$ (e.g. $1 \notin K$), so this satisfies the question.


**(d)** Write a formula in the logic of predicates asserting that $\leq$ is not a partial order on the set $P$. The formula can use existential quantifiers $\exists$ only (with variables ranging over $P$), any Boolean connectives, and atomic formulas of the form "$x \leq y$" and "$x = y$". You may assume that $P \neq \emptyset$.

>[!hint]- Hint 1
> $\leq$ is a partial order iff it is **reflexive**, **antisymmetric**, and **transitive**. First write that as a conjunction of three universally-quantified statements.

>[!hint]- Hint 2
> Now negate. Push the negation through using:
> - $\neg(\forall x.\, P) \;\equiv\; \exists x.\, \neg P$ (quantifier negation)
> - $\neg(A \land B) \;\equiv\; \neg A \lor \neg B$ (De Morgan's)
> - $\neg(A \to B) \;\equiv\; A \land \neg B$ (negating implication)
>
> Apply these to each clause of the partial-order conjunction.

>[!check]- Solution
> $\leq$ is a partial order on $P$ iff:
> 1. $\forall x.\, x \leq x$ (reflexive)
> 2. $\forall x \forall y.\, (x \leq y \land y \leq x) \to x = y$ (antisymmetric)
> 3. $\forall x \forall y \forall z.\, (x \leq y \land y \leq z) \to x \leq z$ (transitive)
>
> "$\leq$ is *not* a partial order" is $\neg((1) \land (2) \land (3)) \equiv \neg(1) \lor \neg(2) \lor \neg(3)$. Negating each clause:
>
> - $\neg(1)$: $\exists x.\, \neg(x \leq x)$
> - $\neg(2)$: $\exists x \exists y.\, (x \leq y \land y \leq x \land \neg(x = y))$
> - $\neg(3)$: $\exists x \exists y \exists z.\, (x \leq y \land y \leq z \land \neg(x \leq z))$
>
> So the desired formula is:
> $(\exists x.\, \neg(x \leq x)) \;\lor\; (\exists x \exists y.\, x \leq y \land y \leq x \land \neg(x = y)) \;\lor\; (\exists x \exists y \exists z.\, x \leq y \land y \leq z \land \neg(x \leq z))$.
>
> This uses only $\exists$, Boolean connectives, and the allowed atomic formulas.


**(e)** Let $\Omega = \{1, 2, 3, 4, 5\}$ be the sample space in a random experiment. Give two events $A$ and $B$ and specify probabilities for all elementary events so that:
- every elementary event has nonzero probability,
- $A$ and $B$ are nonempty, different from $\Omega$, and independent.

If this is not possible, explain why.

>[!hint]- Hint 1
> Recall: $A$ and $B$ are **independent** iff $P(A \cap B) = P(A) \cdot P(B)$.

>[!hint]- Hint 2
> Pick a small overlapping pair like $A = \{1, 2\}$, $B = \{1, 3\}$ so $A \cap B = \{1\}$. Independence then reduces to one equation: $P(\{1\}) = P(A) \cdot P(B)$. You have lots of freedom in choosing the elementary probabilities to satisfy it.

>[!check]- Solution
> Yes, this is possible. Take $A = \{1, 2\}$, $B = \{1, 3\}$, with elementary probabilities
> $P(\{1\}) = P(\{2\}) = P(\{3\}) = \tfrac{1}{4}, \qquad P(\{4\}) = P(\{5\}) = \tfrac{1}{8}$.
>
> All five elementary events have nonzero probability, and the total is $3 \cdot \tfrac{1}{4} + 2 \cdot \tfrac{1}{8} = \tfrac{3}{4} + \tfrac{1}{4} = 1$.
>
> $P(A) = \tfrac{1}{4} + \tfrac{1}{4} = \tfrac{1}{2}$, and likewise $P(B) = \tfrac{1}{2}$. Then $P(A) \cdot P(B) = \tfrac{1}{4} = P(\{1\}) = P(A \cap B)$, so $A$ and $B$ are independent.
>
> Both events are nonempty and neither equals $\Omega$.


**(f)** Give an example of a finite sample space $\Omega$ and a function $Q: 2^\Omega \to \mathbb{R}$ such that $Q(\Omega) = 1$, $Q(A \cup B) = Q(A) + Q(B)$ whenever $A \cap B = \emptyset$, but $Q$ is not a probability measure; in other words, $Q(A) < 0$ for some $A \subseteq \Omega$. If this is not possible, explain why.

>[!hint]- Hint 1
> Try the smallest interesting $\Omega$: just two elements. Finite additivity then determines $Q$ entirely from its values on the two singletons, and the constraint $Q(\Omega) = 1$ just says those two values sum to $1$. There's no positivity requirement built into either constraint, so you have room to make one of them negative.

>[!check]- Solution
> Take $\Omega = \{1, 2\}$ and define
> $Q(\emptyset) = 0, \quad Q(\{1\}) = -1, \quad Q(\{2\}) = 2, \quad Q(\Omega) = 1$.
>
> Then:
> - $Q(\Omega) = 1$.> - $Q$ is finitely additive: the only nontrivial disjoint pair is $\{1\}$ and $\{2\}$, and $Q(\{1\} \cup \{2\}) = Q(\Omega) = 1 = (-1) + 2 = Q(\{1\}) + Q(\{2\})$.> - $Q(\{1\}) = -1 < 0$, so $Q$ is not a probability measure (probability measures require non-negativity).

3) **(a)** Is the composition of two equivalence relations always an equivalence relation? Give a proof or a counterexample.

>[!hint]- Hint 1
> Recall: a relation is an equivalence relation iff it is **reflexive**, **symmetric**, and **transitive**. Composition $A \cdot B$ is defined by $x \,(A \cdot B)\, y \iff \exists c.\, x A c \land c B y$.

>[!hint]- Hint 2
> Try to prove each of reflexivity, symmetry, and transitivity for $A \cdot B$ in turn (assuming both $A$ and $B$ are equivalence relations on the same set). Two of them go through cleanly. One doesn't, that's where the counterexample will come from.

>[!hint]- Hint 3
> Symmetry is the problem.
>
> Suppose $x \,(A \cdot B)\, y$, so there is some $c$ with $x A c$ and $c B y$. Symmetry of $A$ and $B$ gives $c A x$ and $y B c$, but rearranging that gives $y \,(B \cdot A)\, x$, *not* $y \,(A \cdot B)\, x$ in general. So symmetry of the composition isn't automatic.

>[!hint]- Hint 4
> To build a counterexample, find equivalence relations $A$ and $B$ on a small set where $x \,(A \cdot B)\, y$ holds (i.e. there's an intermediate $c$ with $x A c$ and $c B y$) but the *reverse* path doesn't exist (no $c'$ with $y A c'$ and $c' B x$). Three elements is enough.

>[!check]- Solution
> No, the composition of two equivalence relations is **not** in general an equivalence relation, because **symmetry** can fail.
>
> **Why symmetry fails in general.** Suppose $x \,(A \cdot B)\, y$, so there is $c$ with $x A c$ and $c B y$. Symmetry of $A$ and $B$ gives $c A x$ and $y B c$, which means $y \,(B \cdot A)\, x$. But $A \cdot B \neq B \cdot A$ in general, so this does not give us $y \,(A \cdot B)\, x$.
>
> **Counterexample.** Let $S = \{1, 2, 3\}$ and define
> - $A = \{(1,1), (2,2), (3,3), (1,2), (2,1)\}$, the equivalence relation with classes $\{1, 2\}$ and $\{3\}$.
> - $B = \{(1,1), (2,2), (3,3), (2,3), (3,2)\}$, the equivalence relation with classes $\{1\}$ and $\{2, 3\}$.
>
> Both are equivalence relations on $S$ (reflexive, symmetric, transitive). Now look at $A \cdot B$:
> - $1 \,(A \cdot B)\, 3$, witnessed by $c = 2$: $1 A 2$ and $2 B 3$.> - But $3 \,(A \cdot B)\, 1$ would require some $c$ with $3 A c$ and $c B 1$. Since $A$'s class containing $3$ is just $\{3\}$, we'd need $c = 3$. Then $3 B 1$ would have to hold, but $B$'s class containing $3$ is $\{2, 3\}$, which does not include $1$. So no such $c$ exists.>
> Hence $1 \,(A \cdot B)\, 3$ but $\neg(3 \,(A \cdot B)\, 1)$, violating symmetry. So $A \cdot B$ is not an equivalence relation.


**(b)** A relation $S$ is a partial order on a finite set $A$, $|A| = k$. For each $k \geq 1$, what is the maximum value that the size $|S|$ can attain? Justify your answer.

>[!hint]- Hint 1
> Think of $S$ as a subset of $A \times A$. Reflexivity forces all $k$ pairs $(x, x)$ to be in $S$. For *distinct* $x \neq y$, what does **antisymmetry** allow regarding $(x, y)$ and $(y, x)$?

>[!hint]- Hint 2
> Antisymmetry says we can have at most *one* of $(x, y)$, $(y, x)$ in $S$ when $x \neq y$. So the maximum is achieved when, for every pair $\{x, y\}$ of distinct elements, exactly one direction is included. That's a **total order**.

>[!check]- Solution
> The maximum is $|S| = \dbinom{k}{2} + k = \dfrac{k(k+1)}{2} = \displaystyle\sum_{i=1}^{k} i$, achieved by any total order on $A$.
>
> **Upper bound:** $S$ is a subset of $A \times A$, so it consists of:
> - Reflexive pairs $(x, x)$: there are $k$ of these. They must all be in $S$ by reflexivity.
> - "Cross" pairs $(x, y)$ with $x \neq y$: there are $k(k-1)$ such ordered pairs, grouped into $\binom{k}{2}$ unordered pairs $\{x, y\}$. For each such pair, antisymmetry rules out having both $(x, y)$ and $(y, x)$ in $S$ (that would force $x = y$). So at most one of the two ordered pairs is in $S$, giving at most $\binom{k}{2}$ cross pairs.
>
> Total: $|S| \leq k + \binom{k}{2} = \dfrac{k(k+1)}{2}$.
>
> **Achievability:** Take $A = \{1, 2, \ldots, k\}$ with the usual ordering, $S = \{(i, j) : i \leq j\}$. This is a partial order (reflexive, antisymmetric, transitive, in fact a total order), and it contains every reflexive pair plus exactly one direction of each cross pair, giving $|S| = \dfrac{k(k+1)}{2}$.
>
> **Hasse-diagram intuition:** A total order's Hasse diagram is a single vertical chain. Counting the elements each vertex relates to (including itself):
> - the bottom element relates to all $k$ elements (itself plus the $k-1$ above it),
> - the second-from-bottom relates to $k - 1$ elements (itself plus the $k-2$ above it),
> - $\ldots$
> - the top element relates only to itself: $1$ element.
>
> Summing gives $k + (k-1) + (k-2) + \cdots + 1 = \dfrac{k(k+1)}{2}$, matching the upper bound.
